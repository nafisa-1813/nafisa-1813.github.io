Prologue

The Parable of the Library

Imagine a vast library where every book is written in a language you've never studied. Now imagine a brilliant librarian who, after reading millions of books, learned to predict what word comes next on any page — and got so impossibly good at it that she seemed to understand the books entirely. She could summarize them, translate them, answer questions about them, write new ones in the same style.

What is she doing internally? Is she running some elaborate grammar rule? Is she keeping a mental map of every sentence she's read? Or has she discovered some deeper structure in language that nobody ever wrote down?

That librarian is a large language model. And the question of what she's doing internally — the exact circuits, the stored facts, the rules she's learned — is the field of mechanistic interpretability.

To understand mech interp, you first need to understand the machine itself. This article is that foundation.

Chapter One
History

The Road to Transformers

The story doesn't begin in 2017 with "Attention Is All You Need." It begins with a much humbler question: can a machine learn that the word "king" relates to "queen" the same way "man" relates to "woman"?

1986
Backpropagation & Neural Language Models
Rumelhart, Hinton & Williams make training deep networks practical via backprop. Bengio et al. (2003) later show that neural networks can learn distributed representations of words — each word mapped to a dense vector of numbers, not a one-hot code. The key insight: nearby vectors mean similar meanings.
2013
Word2Vec — Geometry of Meaning
Mikolov et al. at Google show that simple neural networks trained to predict context words produce stunning vector arithmetic: vec(king) − vec(man) + vec(woman) ≈ vec(queen). Language has geometry. But these are static embeddings — every word has one fixed vector regardless of context.
1997 – 2015
RNNs & LSTMs — Memory Through Time
Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) invent the Long Short-Term Memory network. As Chris Olah's famous blog explains: RNNs process sequences step by step, passing a hidden state like a baton. LSTMs add gating mechanisms (forget gate, input gate, output gate) so the network can choose what to remember and what to discard. They work well — but slowly, and they forget things from far back in long sequences.
2014 – 2015
Seq2Seq & the Birth of Attention
Sutskever et al. introduce encoder-decoder RNNs for translation. Bahdanau et al. (2015) add the crucial twist: instead of squeezing the entire source sentence into one vector, let the decoder "attend" to different parts of the input at each step. Attention is born. It fixes the bottleneck problem — but it's still bolted onto RNNs.
2017
"Attention Is All You Need" — The Transformer
Vaswani, Shazeer, Parmar, Uszkoreit et al. at Google Brain ask a radical question: what if we removed the RNN entirely and just used attention? The result — the Transformer — is parallelizable, scalable, and better at capturing long-range dependencies. It dominates NLP within a year.
2018
GPT & BERT — The Pretrain-Finetune Paradigm
OpenAI's GPT (decoder-only, trained to predict next word) and Google's BERT (encoder-only, trained on masked tokens) demonstrate that pretraining on massive text corpora then fine-tuning on specific tasks is extraordinarily powerful. Transfer learning arrives in NLP.
2019 – 2020
GPT-2, GPT-3 — Scale Is All You Need (Too)
OpenAI shows that scaling — more parameters, more data, more compute — keeps improving performance without changing the basic architecture. GPT-3 with 175B parameters can do few-shot learning from examples in the prompt alone. The "emergent abilities" debate begins.
2020 – 2021
Mechanistic Interpretability Begins
Chris Olah and colleagues at Anthropic begin publishing the Circuits thread, reverse-engineering individual neurons and circuits in neural networks. Elhage et al. (2021) publish "A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits" — the first systematic attempt to understand what computations transformers implement, at the level of weights and activations.
2022 – 2023
RLHF, ChatGPT, Superposition & SAEs
InstructGPT/ChatGPT show that Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback dramatically improves alignment and usability. Meanwhile, Elhage et al. (2022) discover "superposition" — neurons represent far more features than their count by overlapping them. Bricken et al. (2023) use Sparse Autoencoders to decompose neurons into individual, interpretable features.
2023 – 2024
Mixture of Experts, Long Context, Multimodality
GPT-4, Claude 3, Gemini introduce multimodal inputs, 100k+ token context windows, and sparse MoE layers. Architectural innovations (Flash Attention, RoPE, GQA, SwiGLU) accumulate to make models faster and capable. The basic Transformer blueprint, however, remains.
2025 –
Circuit Tracing & Attribution Graphs
Ameisen et al. (2025) develop "circuit tracing" — automated methods to trace the step-by-step computation path in a model responding to a prompt. Anthropic's interpretability team finds emotion representations, introspective awareness, and specific circuits for factual recall in production models.
Chapter Two
Architecture Overview

The Machine, From 10,000 Feet

Before we zoom in to the nuts and bolts, let's establish the whole picture. A language model takes a sequence of tokens (roughly: words or word-pieces) and outputs a probability distribution over what token comes next. That's it. The extraordinary capabilities emerge from learning to do this extremely well on vast amounts of text.

The Transformer does this through a pipeline of transformations, each layer passing a refined understanding of the input to the next. Think of it as an assembly line where each station adds something — context, relationships, factual associations — to a representation that gets progressively richer.

FIG 1 — High-level Transformer architecture. The residual stream is the central highway; attention and FFN are write operations on it.

The key metaphor that mech interp researchers use, from Elhage et al. (2021): think of the model as having a residual stream — a vector that flows through the model and gets progressively updated. Each attention layer and each feed-forward layer reads from and writes to this stream. The stream starts as the embedding and ends as the input to the unembedding (which produces word probabilities).

This framing is enormously useful for understanding the model. The residual stream is the model's "working memory" at each position, and all the interesting computation is about what different layers choose to write into it.

Chapter Three
Step 1

Tokenization & Embeddings — Giving Words a Body

Language is made of characters. Neural networks need numbers. The bridge between them has two steps: tokenization and embedding.

Tokenization: Breaking Text Into Atoms

Modern models use Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) — a compression algorithm repurposed for this task. The idea: start with individual characters, then repeatedly merge the most common adjacent pair into a single token. Run this long enough and you end up with a vocabulary of ~50,000 tokens that include common words ("the", "is", "transformers"), word fragments ("##ing", "un##"), punctuation, and even some multi-word units.

The sentence "The cat sat on the mat" might become tokens: ["The", " cat", " sat", " on", " the", " mat"] — roughly one per word here, but longer words get split: "tokenization" → ["token", "ization"].

Why This Matters for Mech Interp
The tokenizer is the boundary between human language and the model's world. One quirk: the model doesn't see characters, it sees tokens. So "100" is one token but "1000" might be two. This causes surprising arithmetic failures and other "bugs" that are really tokenization artifacts — an important first layer of mechanistic analysis.

Embedding: Giving Tokens a Location in Space

Each token in the vocabulary is assigned a d_model-dimensional vector (e.g. d_model = 512 for the original Transformer, 12,288 for GPT-3). This is the embedding matrix W_E ∈ ℝ^(vocab_size × d_model). You look up row i to get the embedding of token i.

These vectors are learned from scratch during training. After training, they encode rich semantic relationships — the geometry Chris Olah calls "representations". Words with similar meanings cluster together; directional vectors encode relationships (plurals, verb tenses, sentiment).

FIG 2 — Left: word vectors cluster by semantic similarity in embedding space. Right: token → d_model vector lookup, plus sinusoidal positional encoding added on top.

Positional Encoding: Where Am I in the Sentence?

Unlike RNNs, Transformers process all tokens simultaneously. They have no inherent sense of order. So we inject positional information by adding a positional encoding vector to each token embedding before passing it to the model.

The original paper used fixed sinusoidal encodings (sines and cosines at different frequencies). Modern models use Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) — which encodes position by rotating the query and key vectors in attention, allowing the model to generalize to longer sequences than it was trained on.

Mech Interp Lens: The Residual Stream Starts Here
After embedding + positional encoding, each token position has a vector in ℝ^d_model. This is the initial state of the residual stream at that position. Every subsequent layer will add to this vector. The final vector, run through the unembedding matrix, gives the distribution over next tokens. Mech interp researchers study: what information gets written into the stream, by whom, and when?
Chapter Four
The Core Mechanism

Attention — The Model Talking to Itself

This is the heart of the Transformer. Everything else is scaffolding. Attention is how tokens communicate with each other.

The intuition: when processing the word "it" in "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired," a human immediately knows "it" refers to "animal," not "street." Attention lets the model figure this out by computing, for each token, how much it should "attend to" every other token.

The Query-Key-Value Framework

Think of attention like a library search system. Each token position broadcasts:

  • A Query (Q): "What am I looking for?" — like a search query.
  • Keys (K): "What do I contain?" — like a book's index entry.
  • Values (V): "What information do I actually hold?" — the book's content.

The attention score between position i (the query) and position j (the key) is the dot product of their vectors. High dot product = high attention = token i pulls heavily from token j's value.

Scaled Dot-Product Attention
Q = X · W_Q (queries: what am I looking for?)
K = X · W_K (keys: what does each token contain?)
V = X · W_V (values: what each token wants to share)

Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax( Q · Kᵀ / √d_k ) · V

where d_k = dimension of key vectors (√d_k prevents dot products from getting too large)
FIG 3 — Attention computation. Left: "it" strongly attends to "animal" (shown by thick arrow, weight 0.72). Right: The attention score matrix — each row sums to 1.0 after softmax. The causal mask prevents each token from attending to future tokens.

Multi-Head Attention: Different Conversations in Parallel

Instead of one set of Q, K, V matrices, the Transformer uses H heads (e.g. H=8 or H=16), each with its own W_Q, W_K, W_V. Each head runs attention independently with lower-dimensional vectors (d_k = d_model / H), then the results are concatenated and projected back to d_model.

Multi-Head Attention
MultiHead(Q, K, V) = Concat(head_1, ..., head_H) · W_O

where head_i = Attention(X·W_Q_i, X·W_K_i, X·W_V_i)

Each head can specialize: one might track syntactic dependencies, another might handle coreference ("it" → "animal"), another might track positional patterns.

This is where mech interp gets exciting. Research by Elhage et al. (2021) and subsequent work shows that individual attention heads really do specialize in identifiable functions.

The Causal Mask

For language modeling (predicting the next word), a token must not be able to see future tokens — that would be cheating. The causal mask sets all scores where j > i to −∞ before the softmax, making their weights effectively zero. After softmax, each token can only attend to itself and previous tokens.

Mech Interp Landmark: Induction Heads
One of the most important discoveries in mech interp (Olsson et al., 2022): induction heads. These are pairs of attention heads that implement a specific algorithm: if the model has seen "A B ... A" in context, an induction head makes it predict "B" will follow the second "A". This is the mechanistic basis of in-context learning — the model's ability to learn new patterns from examples given in the prompt. Induction heads emerge at a specific training step and their emergence causes a phase transition in in-context learning ability.
Chapter Five
Step 3

The Feed-Forward Network — The Model's Memory Bank

After attention mixes information across positions, each token's representation goes through a feed-forward network (FFN) — applied independently to each position. Think of attention as the model communicating between positions; the FFN as the model "thinking" at each position individually.

The FFN is a simple two-layer MLP:

Feed-Forward Network
FFN(x) = W_2 · GELU( W_1 · x + b_1 ) + b_2

where W_1 ∈ ℝ^(d_model × 4·d_model) (expand to 4× width)
W_2 ∈ ℝ^(4·d_model × d_model) (project back)

The hidden dimension is 4× larger — a "bottleneck" that was first introduced in the original paper. Modern models use variants: SwiGLU (Llama), GeGLU (PaLM) for better gradient flow.
FIG 4 — The Feed-Forward Network expands to 4× the model width, applies a nonlinear activation (GELU), then projects back. The skip connection adds the FFN output to the original input.

What Are Neurons Actually Storing?

Here's where it gets philosophically interesting. Early mech interp research hoped that each neuron would correspond to one interpretable concept — one neuron for "France," one for "past tense," one for "negation." The reality is stranger.

Geva et al. (2021) showed that FFN layers act like key-value memories: each row of W_1 is a "key" that pattern-matches against the input, and the corresponding row of W_2 is a "value" that gets added to the residual stream when activated. Some neurons did correspond to interpretable facts: a "Rome neuron" that fires for inputs related to Rome and writes "Italy" into the residual stream (making the model more likely to output "Italian").

But most neurons are polysemantic: they respond to multiple unrelated concepts. Elhage et al. (2022) explain why in their superposition hypothesis — see Section 8.

Chapter Six
The Glue

Residual Connections & Layer Normalization

The Residual Stream: Why It's Central

The original ResNet paper (He et al., 2016) introduced residual connections for vision: instead of learning a transformation F(x), learn the residual F(x) + x. This makes it easier to train deep networks because gradients flow directly through the identity connection.

In Transformers, this creates the residual stream — a vector at each position that flows through the entire model. Every layer adds to it rather than replacing it. The attention and FFN layers are not sequential transformations; they're parallel writers on a shared whiteboard.

Transformer Layer with Residuals (Pre-LayerNorm version)
x₁ = x₀ + Attention(LayerNorm(x₀)) ← attention writes to stream
x₂ = x₁ + FFN(LayerNorm(x₁)) ← FFN writes to stream

Note: modern models (GPT-2, Llama, etc.) use Pre-LayerNorm (normalize before each sublayer), not Post-LayerNorm as in the original paper. Pre-LN trains more stably.

Layer Normalization: Keeping the Stream Stable

Without normalization, activations in deep networks either explode or vanish during training. Layer Norm (Ba et al., 2016) fixes this by normalizing the activations across the d_model dimension at each position:

Layer Normalization
LayerNorm(x) = γ · (x − μ) / (σ + ε) + β

μ = mean of x σ = std dev of x
γ, β = learned scale and shift parameters
ε = small constant for numerical stability (e.g. 1e-5)

Each token's vector is normalized to have mean≈0 and std≈1, then rescaled by learned γ and β.
FIG 5 — The residual stream as a highway. Attention and FFN layers both read from and write to it via vector addition. Early layers build syntactic representations; later layers handle semantics and factual recall.
Why the Residual Stream Framework Is So Powerful for Mech Interp
Because all components add to the residual stream (rather than transforming it sequentially), we can decompose the final prediction as a sum of contributions from each layer, each head, and each FFN. This is the basis of logit lens analysis (Nostalgebraist, 2020) — you can "read off" the stream at any layer to see what the model would predict if it stopped computing there. It's also the foundation of attribution graphs (Ameisen et al., 2025), which trace which specific components caused a specific output.
Chapter Seven
Putting It Together

Stacking Layers: What Each Layer Learns

A GPT-class model stacks N identical layers. GPT-2 Small has 12; GPT-3 has 96; Claude 3 has hundreds. What does this depth buy?

The empirical pattern, validated by mech interp research:

Early Layers (0–25%)

Syntax & Local Patterns

Attention heads track basic syntactic relationships: subject-verb agreement, pronoun resolution at short range, punctuation structure. The residual stream builds a local "parse tree" of the sentence.

Middle Layers (25–65%)

Semantics & Context

Attention heads track long-range semantic dependencies. The FFN layers begin storing and retrieving factual associations ("Rome" → Italy, dates, historical facts). Entity types crystallize.

Later Layers (65–90%)

Task-Specific Reasoning

Higher-level computations: sentiment, entailment, multi-step reasoning chains. These layers refine the representation toward a specific output type. Most "reasoning circuits" discovered in mech interp live here.

Final Layers (90–100%)

Output Shaping

The model formats its representation for the unembedding. Final LayerNorm stabilizes. The last few attention heads often implement "attention sinks" — attending to the [BOS] token as a garbage collector for irrelevant information.

The Unembedding: From Vector to Words

After N layers, the residual stream at position T (the last token) contains a rich summary of everything the model computed. The unembedding matrix W_U ∈ ℝ^(d_model × vocab_size) projects this vector into vocabulary space. Each row of W_U is a direction in d_model space corresponding to one vocabulary item.

Logits and Probabilities
logits = LayerNorm(x_final) · W_U (shape: vocab_size)
probs = softmax(logits / temperature)

Temperature > 1 flattens the distribution (more creative/random). Temperature → 0 makes the model always pick the top token (greedy). Top-p sampling and top-k sampling are additional filtering techniques.

One beautiful symmetry: W_E (the embedding) and W_U (the unembedding) are often tied — they share weights (W_U = W_E^T). This means the model uses the same geometric space for input and output: the direction that strongly represents "France" in embedding space is also the direction that makes the model likely to output "France."

Chapter Eight
Mechanistic Interpretability

Superposition — Why Neurons Are Polysemantic

Here's the puzzle that consumed a large chunk of mech interp research: if you look at any neuron in a large language model, it fires for multiple unrelated concepts. A neuron that responds to "dogs" also fires for "astronomy" and "18th-century French literature." This is called polysemanticity.

Why? Elhage et al. (2022) give the answer: superposition.

The Geometry of Features

Suppose the model wants to represent F features (concepts, facts, properties) but only has d dimensions in its residual stream. If F > d, they can't all get their own orthogonal direction. But: if features are sparse (most are near-zero most of the time), the model can pack many features into the same d dimensions by overlapping them at small angles.

FIG 6 — Superposition: more features than dimensions, packed by rotating feature directions slightly off-orthogonal. Works because real-world concepts are sparse — most aren't active simultaneously.

Sparse Autoencoders: Unscrambling the Superposition

Bricken et al. (2023) and Templeton et al. (2024) found the key tool for fighting superposition: Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs). The idea is elegant. Train a network to:

  • Encode: project the residual stream vector into a much larger space (e.g. 16× the model width)
  • Enforce sparsity: only a few of these larger-space neurons can be active at once (L1 penalty)
  • Decode: reconstruct the original vector from the sparse representation

After training, each of the "superfeature" neurons in the SAE tends to correspond to exactly one human-interpretable concept. Templeton et al. found features for "the Golden Gate Bridge," "DNA transcription," specific programming bugs, and remarkably — features that appear causally relevant to safety-relevant behaviors.

The Golden Gate Claude Experiment
In 2024, Anthropic researchers demonstrated that by artificially activating the "Golden Gate Bridge" feature to an extreme level, the model began claiming to be the Golden Gate Bridge and relating everything to it — a striking demonstration that these features are causally active in determining behavior, not just correlated with it.
Chapter Nine
Architectural Evolution

How the Transformer Changed Between 2017 and 2025

The 2017 Transformer paper introduced a blueprint. What followed was eight years of incremental improvements, each solving a specific scaling or performance problem. Here's the changelog:

Normalization: Post → Pre → RMSNorm

The original paper placed LayerNorm after each sublayer (post-LN). GPT-2 moved it before (pre-LN), stabilizing training at large scale. LLaMA (2023) adopted RMSNorm — dropping the mean subtraction, just scaling by RMS — which is slightly faster and equally effective.

Positional Encoding: Sinusoidal → Learned → RoPE → ALiBi

Original sinusoids were replaced with learned absolute positions (GPT-2). Then Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) — which encodes relative positions by rotating Q and K vectors — became dominant (GPT-NeoX, LLaMA, Mistral). ALiBi (Attention with Linear Biases) is an alternative that adds position-based biases to attention scores, excelling at extrapolation to long contexts.

Activation Functions: ReLU → GELU → SwiGLU

ReLU (the original) was replaced by GELU (smoother, better in practice). SwiGLU (Shazeer, 2020), used in PaLM and LLaMA, replaces the FFN with a gated structure: FFN(x) = (W_1·x ⊙ SiLU(W_3·x)) · W_2. The gating lets the network control information flow within the FFN itself, improving performance substantially.

Attention Efficiency: Standard → Flash Attention → GQA

Standard attention requires O(n²) memory for sequence length n (storing the full attention matrix). Flash Attention (Dao et al., 2022) recomputes attention scores on-the-fly using IO-aware algorithms, reducing memory to O(n) without changing the math. Grouped Query Attention (GQA) — used in LLaMA-2, Mistral — reduces the number of K/V heads while keeping more Q heads, cutting KV cache size dramatically during inference.

Architecture Variants: Encoder-Only, Decoder-Only, Encoder-Decoder

Decoder-Only (GPT, LLaMA, Claude)

Autoregressive Generation

Uses causal (left-to-right) attention. Each token predicts the next. Trained on next-token prediction. Used for generation tasks: chat, coding, writing.

Encoder-Only (BERT, RoBERTa)

Bidirectional Understanding

Sees the full sequence. Trained with masked token prediction (random tokens are masked; model predicts them). Used for classification, named entity recognition, embeddings.

Encoder-Decoder (T5, BART)

Sequence-to-Sequence

Encoder processes the input; decoder generates the output with cross-attention to the encoder. Used for translation, summarization. Now mostly replaced by decoder-only.

Mixture of Experts (Mixtral, GPT-4)

Sparse Activation

The FFN is replaced by N expert FFNs, but only K are activated per token (e.g. 2 of 8). This multiplies parameter count without multiplying compute. The router learned to specialize experts by domain.

Chapter Ten
Training

How the Model Learns

The Objective: Next Token Prediction

The training objective is deceptively simple: given a text corpus, for each position in each document, predict the next token. The loss is cross-entropy between the predicted distribution and the true token:

Language Modeling Objective
L = - (1/T) Σ log P(token_t | token_1, ..., token_{t-1})

Minimizing this over billions of tokens forces the model to learn grammar, facts, reasoning, code, and everything else that helps predict the next word. The magic is that this one objective, applied at scale, produces general intelligence.

Backpropagation Through the Residual Stream

Training uses gradient descent with backpropagation. The key insight Chris Olah emphasized: because of the residual stream, gradients flow directly from loss back through every layer without vanishing. Each attention head and FFN receives clean gradient signal about how it should update its weights.

The Chinchilla Scaling Laws

Hoffmann et al. (2022) discovered that optimal training balances model size N and training tokens D: the compute-optimal ratio is roughly D ≈ 20×N. Before Chinchilla, models were undertrained (too many parameters, too few tokens). LLaMA (2023) aggressively applied this: train a smaller model on far more tokens to get equal or better performance at inference.

From Pretraining to Alignment: RLHF

A pretrained model predicts text. It might complete "How do I make a bomb?" with "Here are the steps..." because that's a plausible continuation from the internet. Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) fixes this by:

  • Collecting human preference data (raters compare model outputs)
  • Training a reward model to score outputs
  • Fine-tuning the LLM with PPO to maximize reward, while staying close to the original (KL penalty)

Anthropic's Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) extends this: instead of human preference labels for all pairs, the model is given a set of principles (a "constitution") and uses AI feedback — the model critiques its own outputs according to the constitution. This scales alignment without requiring humans to rate every response.

Chapter Eleven
The Frontier

Mechanistic Interpretability: Reverse Engineering the Machine

Now that you understand how Transformers work architecturally, you're ready to understand what mech interp is actually doing when it "looks inside" the model.

The Core Project

Elhage et al. (2021) put it beautifully in their framework paper: the goal is to understand a neural network the way an engineer understands a computer program — not just what it does, but how it does it, at the level of individual computations.

The analogy: when you see a computer output "2 + 2 = 4," you know that somewhere in the hardware, there's an adder circuit. You could trace the signal path. In mech interp, we're trying to find the "adder circuits" for things like "knowing that Paris is in France" or "detecting that a sentence expresses anger."

The Mathematical Framework (Elhage et al., 2021)

The framework decomposes the transformer into a sum of terms. The key insight: every attention head can be understood as writing a low-rank update to the residual stream, mediated by a specific pair of matrices. For a single attention head:

Attention Head as a Residual Stream Writer
head_h(x) = A_h · x · W_V_h · W_O_h

where A_h = softmax(x·W_Q_h·W_K_h^T·x^T / √d_k) is the attention pattern
and W_V_h · W_O_h = W_OV_h is the "OV circuit" (what gets written)

The OV circuit tells you WHAT information moves from source to destination. The QK circuit (W_Q·W_K^T) tells you WHERE attention focuses. These two circuits can be analyzed independently.

Key Discoveries in Mech Interp

2021
Mathematical Framework + Virtual Weights
Elhage et al. show that because of the residual stream, attention heads in different layers can "compose" — the output of one head becomes the query/key/value for a later head. This means circuits can span multiple layers and are richer than individual heads.
2022
Induction Heads & In-Context Learning
Olsson et al. show that induction heads (pairs of attention heads implementing pattern-matching) are the primary mechanism behind in-context learning. Their emergence during training coincides precisely with a phase transition in the loss curve — evidence of a genuine mechanistic change.
2022
Superposition & Toy Models
Elhage et al. prove that neural networks can represent far more features than dimensions via superposition, explain polysemanticity, and demonstrate the conditions (feature sparsity, importance) that determine when superposition occurs. Foundational for all subsequent SAE work.
2023
Towards Monosemanticity — SAEs on 1-Layer Transformers
Bricken et al. apply sparse autoencoders to a one-layer transformer, extracting thousands of clean, interpretable features. Features include: "academic writing," "base64 encoding," "DNA sequences," "dates." Many neurons that appeared polysemantic decompose into a small number of monosemantic features.
2024
Scaling Monosemanticity — Claude 3 Sonnet
Templeton et al. scale SAEs to Claude 3 Sonnet, extracting over 34 million features. Notable: features for specific people, places, concepts. Safety-relevant features for bias, deception. The "Dark Triad" features. Causally implicated: suppressing a feature changes model behavior predictably.
2025
Circuit Tracing & Attribution Graphs
Ameisen et al. build "attribution graphs" — automated traces of which features activated which attention heads which activated which FFN neurons, for a specific prompt. For the first time, full computation paths for specific behaviors can be identified. Applied to study: planning, multi-hop reasoning, refusals.

Open Problems in Mech Interp

Despite the remarkable progress, the field is still young. Key open problems as of 2025:

Problem 1

Scalability

SAEs trained on small models don't straightforwardly transfer to frontier models. Methods that work on GPT-2 may not work on Claude 3. Scaling laws for interpretability itself are not well understood.

Problem 2

Completeness

Even with SAEs, we likely recover only a fraction of the features the model uses. There may be "dark matter" features — computations that don't decompose into clean monosemantic features at all.

Problem 3

Faithfulness

Do our explanations accurately describe what the model is computing, or are they post-hoc rationalizations? Ameisen et al. (2025) tackle this with circuit tracing, but verification remains hard.

Problem 4

High-Level Concepts

We can find features for "Paris" but can we find the circuit for "deceptive intent"? Safety-relevant high-level abstractions may be distributed across many features in complex ways that resist simple decomposition.

Epilogue
Closing

The Librarian, Revisited

Remember our librarian? Now we know something about what she's actually doing.

She's built a 12,288-dimensional space where every word and concept has a location. When she reads a new sentence, she runs it through dozens of layers of attention heads and feed-forward networks — each one a small specialised subroutine — adding layer by layer to a running summary of what the text means. Some attention heads track who's speaking to whom. Some FFN neurons fire when they recognize a famous historical date. Some later layers compute whether the sentence is a question, a claim, or a command.

And when she "predicts the next word," she's really projecting that rich summary back into word-space and sampling from the distribution over what word usually comes next in that context, across the billions of texts she was trained on.

The field of mechanistic interpretability is the project of mapping this internal world — not just knowing that she predicts well, but knowing which circuits fire when she answers a question about geography, which features activate when she detects a harmful request, and why she sometimes gets things wrong.

That map doesn't exist yet — not fully. But the tools are sharper every year, and the stakes, for building AI we can trust, are as high as they've ever been.
